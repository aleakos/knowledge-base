# Characterization of Carbon AS4/PEKK Mechanical Properties

## Manufacturing and Testing

Flat laminates were produced by compression molding using the Wabash press. Processing parameters (temperature, pressure, dwell time) were tuned to avoid:
- Fiber fracture due to excessive processing pressure
- Resin-rich areas from insufficient pressure
- Void content

Samples were polished for micrograph preparation using a defined sequence of abrasive grits. Tensile tests were performed on an MTS machine with strain gauges for axial and transverse strain measurement.

## 0° (Fiber Direction) Laminates

- Highest stiffness and strength in the fiber direction
- Failure mode: fiber fracture followed by delamination
- Poisson's ratio varies with increasing axial strain (non-linear behavior)
- Micrograph shows well-consolidated fiber arrangement

## 90° (Transverse Direction) Laminates

- Much lower strength than 0° direction
- Failure mode: crack propagation parallel to fibers and matrix-fiber debonding
- Fracture is more catastrophic (brittle) than 0° laminates
- Micrograph reveals transverse crack paths through matrix

## Quasi-Isotropic Laminates [0/45/90/-45]s

- Intermediate properties between 0° and 90°
- Stress-strain curve shows progressive failure sequence
- Multiple fracture events visible before final failure
- Good micrograph quality achievable with proper mold design (debulking technique)

## ±45° Laminates

- High shear strain capacity
- Large deformation possible before fracture
- Fiber rotation observed during test — contributes to non-linear response
- Delamination at free edges possible

## ±30° Laminates

- Significant fiber rotation under tension (measured via strain gauges)
- Fracture occurs at lower strain than ±45°
- [30°/Resin/-30°/Resin]₂s layup (with resin interlayers) achieves higher failure strain than standard ±30° — resin layers allow fiber rotation

## ±30°/±45° Combined Laminates

- Delamination occurs between ±30° and ±45° layers at moderate strain
- Delamination acts as a strain relief mechanism, increasing global failure strain
- This mechanism is exploited in the tube design

## ±25° and ±20° Laminates

- Low angle laminates approach fiber-dominated behavior
- Failure strain decreases with decreasing angle
- [±20°]₂S fractures in multiple pieces — explosive failure

## Key Summary: Failure Strain vs. Layup

| Layup | Approximate Failure Strain |
|---|---|
| 0° | ~1.5% |
| [±45°]₂s | ~3–4% |
| [±30°]₂s | ~2% |
| [30°/Resin/-30°]₂s | ~3–4% |
| [±30°/±45°]s | ~3% |
| QI [0/45/90/-45]s | ~1.5–2% |

**Key finding**: Laminates with medium-angle fibers (±30°–±45°) and/or resin interlayers show the highest failure strains, making them candidates for large-deformation tube designs.

The theoretical prediction from progressive failure analysis (using CLT with ply-discount method) was validated against experimental results, showing good agreement for stress-strain response up to final failure.
