# Introduction

## Background

The research originated from a 2004 kick-off meeting at Concordia University's Center for Composites (CONCOM), involving Bell Helicopter, Bombardier, NRC-AMTC, and ETS. The goal was to develop a thermoplastic composite landing gear for helicopters — a world-first if achieved.

Most helicopter landing gears are made of high-strength aluminum. The cross tubes serve to absorb energy in hard landings through plastic deformation, while skid tubes provide stability. The key design challenge is balancing energy absorption (requiring high stress) against fatigue life (requiring lower stress).

## Design Challenges with Aluminum Landing Gear

1. **Fabrication cost**: Cross tubes require acid-etching (chemical milling) to achieve varying wall thickness (16 mm thick to 3 mm thin). This process is increasingly restricted by environmental legislation and is performed by only a handful of specialized companies.

2. **Corrosion**: Spot corrosion is a primary failure mode. Internal corrosion is hard to detect, creates stress concentrations, and can lead to abrupt fracture during harsh landings.

3. **Fatigue**: Aluminum (and all nonferrous alloys) has no endurance limit — fatigue will eventually occur regardless of stress magnitude. Shot-peening and cold-worked holes are used to retard crack growth.

4. **Residual strain**: Yield strain for aluminum is typically below 0.7%. Permanent deformation accumulates after repeated landings, increasing maintenance costs.

## Research Objective

Design a composite tube in straight form that demonstrates the same:
- Strength
- Stiffness
- Failure strain (deformability)

...as an equivalent Aluminum 7075-T6 tube. A successful result would enable Bell Helicopter to advance to the next phase: curved composite cross tubes for actual landing gear.

## Research Strategy

1. Select appropriate composite material
2. Characterize mechanical properties
3. Perform stress analysis (analytical and numerical via ANSYS)
4. Design, manufacture, and test composite tube prototypes
5. Extract design guidelines

The research concluded that delamination — normally undesirable — could be exploited as an energy dissipation mechanism during harsh landings, analogous to the plastic deformation of aluminum.
