# Chapters 3–5: Oil and Gas Industry Role, Market Potential, and Policy

## Chapter 3: The Oil and Gas Industry and Geothermal

### Overlapping Competencies

Up to **80% of geothermal project investment** involves skills, technologies, and supply chains directly transferable from the oil and gas sector:

| Oil and Gas Competency | Geothermal Application |
|---|---|
| Directional and deep drilling | Accessing deep geothermal reservoirs |
| Well completion and cementing | High-temperature well construction |
| Reservoir modelling and simulation | EGS design and fluid flow prediction |
| Seismic surveys and subsurface characterisation | Resource assessment and risk reduction |
| Project finance and large-scale management | Bankable geothermal project structures |
| Fluid handling and processing | Brine management and power plant operations |

### Cost Reduction Through Technology Transfer

If oil and gas industry expertise is applied to geothermal:
- Drilling cost learning curves can follow historical oil and gas well cost reductions.
- Key lever: increase **rate of penetration (ROP)** — currently much lower in hard crystalline rock than in sedimentary formations drilled by oil and gas.
- Standardisation of well designs and supply chains reduces engineering costs.

Estimated impact: geothermal LCOE could fall to **~USD 50/MWh** for next-generation projects by 2035 with oil and gas industry engagement.

### Workforce Implications

- Many current geothermal workers came from oil and gas.
- University enrolments in petroleum engineering and related fields have declined in advanced economies — a potential skills bottleneck.
- Apprenticeships and retraining programmes, especially in regions where oil and gas employment is declining, are recommended.

## Chapter 4: Next-Generation Geothermal Market Potential

### Electricity Generation

By region, highest next-generation geothermal electricity market potential:
1. **China** — largest single market; coal transition creates demand for dispatchable clean power.
2. **United States** — established geothermal sector, significant resource, policy support.
3. **India** — fast-growing electricity demand, complement to solar.
4. Together: ~75% of global next-generation geothermal electricity market potential.

Geothermal provides unique value as **dispatchable, firm, low-carbon power** complementing variable renewables (solar PV, wind) without storage penalties.

### Heat Applications

- District heating: geothermal can decarbonise existing fossil fuel district heat networks (notably China, central/eastern Europe).
- Industrial process heat: geothermal suited for low-to-medium temperature processes (<200°C) — food processing, textiles, greenhouse agriculture.
- Global geothermal heat market potential from sedimentary aquifers: **~320 TW** at >90°C.

### Energy Storage

Geothermal reservoirs can serve as underground thermal energy storage (UTES):
- Inject cooler surface water in summer, extract heated fluid in winter.
- Enables seasonal storage to balance heating demand.

### Grid Flexibility

Geothermal power plants can modulate output to provide:
- Frequency regulation.
- Ramping services.
- Contributions to grid adequacy that reduce need for peaking gas plants.

### Brine and Critical Materials

Geothermal brines in some regions (Salton Sea, US; lithium-rich European fields) contain recoverable concentrations of:
- **Lithium** — battery supply chain.
- **Silica, zinc, manganese** — industrial minerals.
- Direct lithium extraction (DLE) from geothermal brine represents a co-production opportunity.

## Chapter 5: Policy Recommendations

### Enabling Ecosystem

- Move geothermal higher on national clean energy policy agendas.
- Develop dedicated geothermal technology roadmaps and targets.
- Recognise geothermal's unique features as dispatchable, firm low-carbon energy.

### Financial Support

- Design **risk mitigation schemes** for early-stage (exploration/drilling) project phases — most analogous to oil and gas exploration risk financing.
- Introduce **long-term revenue certainty** through contracts for difference (CfDs) or power purchase agreements (PPAs).
- Ensure remuneration frameworks reflect geothermal's capacity and flexibility value.

### Permitting

- **Streamline and consolidate** permitting processes; set maximum timelines.
- Create **dedicated geothermal permitting regimes** separate from mining regulations.
- Actively engage communities; establish robust environmental safeguards.

### Research and Innovation

- Expand geothermal-specific R&D programmes.
- Fund demonstration projects for EGS and next-generation drilling.
- Develop open data repositories for subsurface resource assessment.

### Jobs and Skills

- Increase geothermal-specific academic programmes and apprenticeships.
- Partner with oil and gas sector on retraining and skills transfer.
- Support international centres of excellence.
