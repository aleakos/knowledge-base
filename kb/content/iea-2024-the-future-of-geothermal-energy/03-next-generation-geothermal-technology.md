# Next-Generation Geothermal Technology and Technical Potential

## Technology Categories

### Conventional (Hydrothermal)
- Relies on naturally occurring hot water/steam in permeable reservoirs
- Location-specific: requires coincidence of heat, fluid, and permeability
- Temperature, permeability, and fluid chemistry define resource quality

### Next-Generation (Reservoir-Independent)
Two main approaches that do not require pre-existing hydrothermal reservoirs:

1. **Enhanced Geothermal Systems (EGS)**
   - Fluid circulated through engineered fractures in hot dry rock
   - Requires hydraulic stimulation to create/enhance permeability
   - Access to deeper, hotter resources beyond conventional reach

2. **Closed-Loop Geothermal Systems (CLGS)** (also called Advanced Geothermal Systems, AGS)
   - Fluid circulates in a sealed loop with no direct contact with formation
   - No stimulation required; eliminates induced seismicity risk
   - Generally lower flow rates and heat extraction than open EGS

### Ground-Source Heat Pumps (GSHP)
- Use near-surface heat (<100 m depth)
- Supply heating/cooling at <200°C
- Do not require high geothermal gradient

## Technical Potential Assessment

Analysis conducted in collaboration with Project InnerSpace using the GeoMap tool.

### Electricity Generation Potential
- **Next-generation geothermal** technical potential: sufficient to meet global electricity demand **140 times over**
- Potential increases significantly with depth access:
  - Resources at >3 km depth: available in nearly all countries
  - Resources below 8 km depth: ~600 TW potential capacity (25-year lifespan)
- Second only to solar PV among renewable technologies in technical potential

### Heat Potential
- **Sedimentary aquifers** at up to 3 km depth and >90°C: ~320 TW globally
  - Compatible with existing fossil fuel-fired district heating networks
- Lower temperature requirements: potential increases ~tenfold (~3,200 TW)
- Sufficient to meet all heat demand in Africa, China, Europe, Southeast Asia, and the United States

## Key Technical Challenges

### Drilling Costs
- Drilling accounts for 30–60% of total project investment
- Cost increases non-linearly with depth (harder rock, higher temperature, more casing)
- New drilling technologies being explored: plasma drilling, millimeter-wave energy drilling

### Reservoir Creation and Management (EGS)
- Hydraulic fracturing must create adequate permeability without inducing harmful seismicity
- Risk of short-circuiting (fluid channeling through limited fracture pathways)
- Uncertainty in fracture propagation prediction

### Thermal Decline
- Heat extraction from a reservoir gradually depletes the local heat source
- Requires careful management; closed loops have inherently lower extraction rates

### High-Temperature Materials
- Equipment must withstand temperatures >200°C and corrosive fluids
- Casing, completion materials, and downhole tools need adaptation

## Investment in Innovation (2019–2024)

Next-generation geothermal has attracted growing venture capital and government R&D funding. Key areas:
- Novel drilling technologies (non-rotary, high-speed)
- High-temperature downhole sensors and telemetry
- Reservoir simulation and machine learning for subsurface characterization
- Closed-loop heat exchanger design optimization
- Well completion techniques adapted from oil and gas

## Key Countries with Next-Generation Projects

Early deployments and demonstration projects in: United States (Fervo Energy, Quaise Energy), United Kingdom, Netherlands, Germany, Switzerland, Iceland, South Korea.
