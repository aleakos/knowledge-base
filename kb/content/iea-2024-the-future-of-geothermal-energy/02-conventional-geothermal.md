# Chapter 1: Conventional Geothermal

## Definition

Conventional geothermal technology exploits naturally occurring hydrothermal resources: heat stored in permeable or fractured rock saturated with fluid (water or steam) beneath an impermeable caprock. Resource quality depends on temperature, permeability, and fluid properties.

## Current Consumption (2023)

- Total final geothermal energy consumption: **~1.4 EJ** in electricity + **~3.6 EJ** in heat.
- Split: **~21% electricity**, **~79% heat** (including district heating and ground-source heat pumps).
- More than 40 countries use geothermal; top 10 account for ~90% of global use.

### Top Consuming Countries

| Country | Primary Use |
|---|---|
| China | Space heating (~50% of global heat use) |
| United States | Electricity + direct use |
| Türkiye | Electricity + agriculture/tourism heating |
| Sweden / Germany | Ground-source heat pumps |
| Iceland | ~50% of national final energy demand from geothermal |
| Indonesia / Philippines / New Zealand / Italy | Predominantly electricity |

## Power Generation

- Geothermal power plant types:
  - **Dry steam**: Steam from reservoir directly drives turbine (>180°C, above saturation).
  - **Flash steam**: Depressurised brine produces steam separated from water for the turbine (>180°C).
  - **Binary cycle (ORC/Kalina)**: Geothermal fluid heats a secondary working fluid with lower boiling point; suitable from ~95°C upward. Lower efficiency but expands resource access.

- Capacity factor: **>75%** globally (2023) — far exceeding wind (~30%) and solar PV (~15%).

## Costs and Investment

- Geothermal LCOE is highly site-dependent:
  - High-quality hydrothermal resources: among the lowest-cost electricity sources.
  - Resources requiring deep drilling or EGS: currently higher cost.
- Drilling represents the largest cost component (40–60% of total project cost for conventional projects).
- Investment in geothermal has been growing; driven by policy support, grid needs, and carbon pricing.

## Jobs

- Geothermal provides **~145,000 direct jobs** globally (2024 estimate).
- Comparable skill requirements to oil and gas (drilling engineers, geologists, reservoir engineers, plant operators).

## Policy Environment

- Policy support significantly lags behind solar PV and wind:
  - Over 100 countries have solar/wind policies.
  - Fewer than 30 countries have implemented geothermal-specific policies.
- Countries with strong policy frameworks (Iceland, Philippines, Kenya, United States) have higher geothermal penetration.
- Permitting bottlenecks: projects can take **up to 10 years** to commission due to multi-agency approval processes and separating geothermal permits from mining regulations.

## IEA Conventional Geothermal Outlook

Under current policies and technology trends:
- Conventional geothermal capacity is projected to grow steadily, driven by established markets.
- Growth is limited by the geographic concentration of high-quality hydrothermal resources (tectonic plate boundaries, volcanic zones, rift zones).
- Scale-up beyond conventional resources requires next-generation technologies (EGS, CLGS) — the subject of subsequent chapters.
