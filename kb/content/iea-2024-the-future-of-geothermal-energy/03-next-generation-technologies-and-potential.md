# Chapter 2: Geothermal Innovation and Technical Potential

## Next-Generation Geothermal Technologies

### Enhanced Geothermal Systems (EGS)

EGS creates engineered reservoirs in hot dry rock by hydraulically fracturing the subsurface to increase permeability. Cold water is injected through one well, heated by contact with rock, and extracted as hot fluid from another well.

Key characteristics:
- Reservoir-independent: not limited to natural hydrothermal zones.
- Requires deep drilling (typically >3 km).
- Main challenges: induced seismicity, fluid loss, adequate heat exchange area.
- Notable projects: Fervo Energy (US), FORGE demonstration project (US DOE).

### Closed-Loop Geothermal Systems (CLGS) / Advanced Geothermal Systems (AGS)

CLGS circulate working fluid through sealed underground pipes or boreholes without fluid exchange with the rock formation. This eliminates induced seismicity risk but reduces heat transfer efficiency.

Key characteristics:
- No fluid injection into rock mass — minimal seismic risk.
- Lower power output per well than EGS but simpler permitting.
- Examples: Eavor-Loop technology (Eavor Technologies, Canada).

### Deep Drilling Innovations

Technologies enabling access to superhot rock (>374°C, supercritical water):
- **Plasma drilling** and **millimeter-wave energy drilling** (GA Drilling, Quaise Energy).
- Target: access supercritical geothermal systems at 5–10 km depth with potential output per well orders of magnitude higher than conventional.

## Global Technical Potential (First-of-Kind Analysis)

This report presents a first-of-kind global geothermal technical potential assessment conducted with Project InnerSpace using the GeoMap tool.

### Electricity Generation Potential

| Depth Range | Potential |
|---|---|
| >3 km (open to next-gen) | Nearly all countries globally |
| <8 km (25-year lifespan) | ~600 TW of capacity |
| All depths combined | Sufficient to meet global electricity demand 140× |

- China, United States, and India hold the **largest electricity potential**, together ~75% of global total.

### Heat Potential

- Sedimentary aquifer heat (depth <3 km, temperature >90°C): **~320 TW** globally.
- This is consistent with the needs of existing fossil-fuel district heating networks (decarbonization opportunity).
- At lower temperature requirements, geothermal heat potential increases ~10-fold.

### Geographic Distribution

- Conventional hydrothermal: concentrated near tectonic plate boundaries, volcanic arcs, rift zones.
- Next-generation: nearly global distribution once depth access is available.
- Regions with highest urgency: Southeast Asia (coal transition), Sub-Saharan Africa (energy access), Europe (grid decarbonization).

## Key Technical Challenges

1. **Drilling cost and speed**: Deep wells are expensive; current cost ~USD 3–10 million per well for 4–5 km depth. Next-generation drilling technologies aim to cut this by 50–90%.

2. **Reservoir creation and management (EGS)**: Stimulation must create sufficient permeability without triggering felt seismicity. Traffic light protocols are used to manage risk.

3. **Well integrity at high temperatures**: Materials, cement, and casing must withstand >300°C conditions.

4. **Subsurface characterization**: Uncertainty in rock properties, fracture networks, and fluid chemistry before drilling creates front-end risk.

5. **Fluid chemistry and scaling**: Geothermal brines can be corrosive and form mineral scale that reduces heat exchanger efficiency.

## Innovation Investment

- Investment in next-generation geothermal R&D has grown significantly since 2020.
- Major investors: US DOE (FORGE project, EGS Collab), EU Horizon programmes, national laboratories.
- Private sector: Fervo, Eavor, Quaise, GA Drilling, and others have attracted venture and corporate investment.
- Oil and gas majors (Shell, Equinor, Repsol, SLB, Halliburton) are increasingly active in geothermal technology development.
