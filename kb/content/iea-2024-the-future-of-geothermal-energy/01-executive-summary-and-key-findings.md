# Executive Summary and Key Findings

## Overview

The IEA's 2024 special report "The Future of Geothermal Energy" finds that geothermal is at a critical juncture. New drilling and reservoir technologies — many adapted from oil and gas — are opening access to previously untapped resources globally, potentially transforming geothermal from a niche regional technology into a mainstream clean energy source.

## Technology Breakthroughs

Advances enabling the expansion of geothermal include:
- **Horizontal drilling** and **hydraulic fracturing** techniques developed by the North American oil and gas industry.
- Enhanced geothermal systems (EGS) that create engineered reservoirs in hot dry rock.
- Closed-loop geothermal systems (CLGS/AGS) that circulate fluid in sealed underground circuits without requiring pre-existing reservoir permeability.

## Global Potential

- The full technical potential of next-generation geothermal for electricity generation is **second only to solar PV** among renewables, sufficient to meet global electricity demand **140 times over**.
- Resources at depths **below 8 km** could deliver close to **600 TW** of geothermal capacity (25-year lifespan basis).
- Sedimentary aquifer heat potential at depths up to 3 km and temperatures >90°C: **~320 TW** globally — consistent with the needs of existing fossil-fuel district heating networks.
- Geothermal potential is sufficient to meet **all electricity and heat demand** in Africa, China, Europe, Southeast Asia, and the United States.

## Current Status (2023 Baseline)

- Geothermal meets less than **1% of global energy demand**.
- Installed geothermal capacity utilization rate: over **75%** in 2023.
  - Compare: wind ~30%, solar PV ~15%.
- Total geothermal final energy use in 2023: **~5 EJ** (~0.8% of global energy demand).
- Top 10 consumers account for ~90% of global total; China alone accounts for ~50% (exclusively space heating).

## Cost Reduction Trajectory

With the right policy and industry support:
- Costs for next-generation geothermal could fall by **~80% by 2035**.
- At that point, new projects could deliver electricity at **~USD 50/MWh**, making geothermal one of the cheapest dispatchable low-emissions sources — competitive with nuclear, hydro, bioenergy, and solar PV + storage.

## Investment Outlook

- Cumulative investment in geothermal could reach **USD 1 trillion by 2035** and **USD 2.5 trillion by 2050**.
- Peak annual investment could reach **USD 140 billion/year** — exceeding current global onshore wind investment.
- Growing interest from technology companies (data centres) seeking dispatchable clean power.

## Market Potential by 2050

With continued technology improvements, geothermal could:
- Meet up to **15% of global electricity demand growth** to 2050.
- Support deployment of up to **800 GW** of geothermal capacity.
- Produce nearly **6 000 TWh/year** (equivalent to combined US + India electricity demand today).

## Oil and Gas Industry Role

Up to **80% of geothermal project investment** involves capabilities common in the oil and gas sector:
- Subsurface characterization and geological assessment.
- Directional drilling and well completion.
- Fluid management and large-scale project execution.

## Key Challenges

1. **Project development risk**: High upfront exploration and drilling costs with uncertain resource confirmation.
2. **Permitting**: Up to a decade to commission a new project; administrative complexity.
3. **Environmental concerns**: Induced seismicity (EGS), fluid management, surface impacts.
4. **Workforce**: ~145,000 geothermal jobs today; could rise 6x to ~1 million by end of this decade, but risk of skills shortfall.
5. **Policy gap**: Over 100 countries have solar/wind policies, but fewer than 30 have geothermal-specific policies.
