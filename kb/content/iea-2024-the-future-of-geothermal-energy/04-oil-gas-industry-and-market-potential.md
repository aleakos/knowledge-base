# Oil and Gas Industry Role and Market Potential

## Overlapping Competencies

Up to **80% of investment** in a geothermal project involves capabilities common to the oil and gas industry:

| Competency | Oil and Gas | Geothermal |
|---|---|---|
| Subsurface characterization | Seismic, well logging | Geothermal surveys, temperature logs |
| Well drilling | Rotary drilling, directional | Same + high-temperature adaptation |
| Well completion | Casing, cementing | Similar; higher temperature requirements |
| Fluid management | Produced water handling | Brine management, reinjection |
| Project management | Large capital projects | Similar scale and complexity |
| Permitting and regulatory | Extensive experience | Less developed frameworks |

## Cost Reduction Through Knowledge Transfer

With full engagement of the oil and gas sector, geothermal drilling costs could fall substantially through:
- Improved rate of penetration (ROP) using PDC bits adapted for hard rock
- Better well planning and trajectory optimization (directional drilling)
- Standardized well designs reducing engineering time and cost
- Supply chain economies of scale

Target: **80% reduction in next-generation geothermal costs by 2035** (from current levels), reaching ~USD 50/MWh.

## Workers and Skills

- Many current geothermal workers came from oil and gas
- University program enrolments in petroleum engineering have declined in advanced economies
- Geothermal employment could rise from ~145,000 today to **1 million by end of decade**
- Risk of skills shortfall without targeted training programs
- Apprenticeships, retraining programs, and regional centres of excellence needed

## Market Potential to 2050

### Electricity

| Region | Relative Market Potential |
|---|---|
| China | Largest |
| United States | Second |
| India | Third |
| Europe | Significant |
| Southeast Asia | Significant (coal transition) |

China, US, and India together: **~75% of global next-generation geothermal electricity potential**

**By 2050 scenarios:**
- Up to **800 GW** deployed under cost-competitive scenario
- ~6,000 TWh/year production
- Investment: USD 2.5 trillion cumulative

### Heat Applications

- **District heating**: geothermal can decarbonize existing fossil fuel-fired networks
- **Industrial heat**: low-to-medium temperature process heat (food, textiles, greenhouses)
- **Direct use**: bathing, aquaculture, agricultural drying

### Energy Storage

- Geothermal reservoirs can act as thermal energy storage
- Enables shifting heat generation to periods of low electricity demand
- Integrates with district heating load management

### Power System Flexibility

- Geothermal can ramp output to compensate for variable wind/solar
- Dispatchable nature provides grid stability services
- Complementary to large solar PV deployments (nighttime/cloudy periods)

### Critical Minerals from Geothermal Brine

- Geothermal fluids in some locations contain economically significant concentrations of:
  - Lithium (Salton Sea, California; Cornish geothermal, UK)
  - Silica
  - Zinc, manganese
- Brine processing as a co-product could improve project economics substantially

## Policy Recommendations

1. Move geothermal up national energy policy agendas with specific targets
2. Implement risk mitigation schemes for early-stage drilling (grants, subsidized loans, public insurance)
3. Introduce long-term revenue certainty (feed-in tariffs, PPAs, auction contracts)
4. Streamline and simplify permitting; consider dedicated geothermal permitting regimes
5. Design robust environmental and social safeguard regulations
6. Support geothermal heat applications with infrastructure financing
7. Create open data repositories for subsurface resource assessment
8. Fund geothermal-specific R&D and demonstration programs
9. Expand academic programs and worker training in geothermal disciplines
10. Promote international technical standards for geothermal development
