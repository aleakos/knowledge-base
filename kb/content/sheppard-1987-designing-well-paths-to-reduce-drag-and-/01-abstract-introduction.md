## Abstract

Deviated wells with undersection trajectories (trajectories lying below conventional tangent sections while constantly building to target) can exhibit lower drag and torque than conventional well geometries under certain circumstances. The paper discusses the influence of well geometry on drag and torque using theoretical models.

Key findings:
- Although undersection wells may have reduced overall drag, side forces in the drill collars increase
- This increased side force can lead to greater danger of sticking in the bottomhole assembly (BHA)
- Enhanced side forces near the bit in undersection wells may also exhibit greater torque
- The relative merits of constant-build and catenary trajectories are compared

## Introduction

### Sources of Drag and Torque Loss

Drag and torque loss are phenomena particularly associated with deviated wells. These can result from:

- **Differential sticking** - pipe sticking due to pressure differential across hole wall
- **Key seating** - pipe wear at formation changes
- **Hole instabilities** - wellbore structural failures
- **Poor hole cleaning** - inadequate cuttings removal
- **Frictional interaction** - side forces along the drillstring

The side-force profile is essentially determined by well geometry, allowing separation of drag problems into:

1. Those caused by poor hole condition or inappropriate mud weight
2. Those associated with the well path itself

### Research Objective

This paper discusses the extent to which drag and torque loss associated with frictional interaction of the drillstring with the wellbore can be reduced by suitable well path design. Previous research suggested catenary well paths reduce torque and drag; this work analyzes that proposal and investigates less exotic solutions.

### Paper Organization

The analysis proceeds through:
1. Development of a friction-based drag and torque model
2. Derivation of drag profiles for planar wells
3. Analysis of undersection trajectory effects
4. Comparison of constant-build vs. catenary trajectories
5. Detailed analysis of side force redistribution
