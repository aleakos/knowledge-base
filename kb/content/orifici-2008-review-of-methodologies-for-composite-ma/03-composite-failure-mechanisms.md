## Overview

Composite materials display a wide variety of failure mechanisms as a result of their complex structure and manufacturing processes, which include fibre failure, matrix cracking, buckling and delamination. Based on these failure mechanisms it can be more appropriate to consider the composite as a structure rather than as a material.

## Fibre Failure

Fibre failure is one of the simplest failure mechanisms to identify and quantify, and occurs when the loads applied to a composite structure cause fracture in the fibres. Fibre failure becomes critical when there are not enough intact fibres remaining to carry the required loads. This mechanism typically dominates in tension-dominated loading conditions.

### Characteristics
- Observable and quantifiable
- Results from tensile overload of fibre material
- Critical when fibre density becomes insufficient to support loads
- Can be analyzed using standard fracture mechanics principles at the fibre level

## Matrix Cracking

Matrix cracks are an intralaminar form of damage, and involve cracks or voids between fibres within a single composite layer, or lamina. This form of damage is complex because it initiates at defects or fibre–matrix interfaces, accumulates throughout the laminate, and can coalesce leading to failure across critical fracture planes.

### Key Aspects
- Initiates typically at defects or fibre–matrix interfaces
- Accumulates gradually throughout the material
- Can coalesce into critical failure planes
- Affected by transverse stresses and shear stresses
- More prevalent in compression-dominated loading

## Buckling

Buckling is a structural phenomenon that occurs in compression or shear, and though not necessarily resulting in failure, the large deformations, bending and loss of structural capacity involved typically promotes other types of damage and leads to structural collapse.

### Structural Implications
- Occurs under compressive or shear loading
- Associated with large deformations
- Reduces effective structural stiffness
- Triggers cascading failure mechanisms
- Critical in lightweight composite structures

## Delamination

Delaminations are separations between internal layers of a composite laminate caused by high through-thickness stresses, and cause significant structural damage, particularly in compression. Delamination can result from:

- Impact damage
- Manufacturing defects
- Stress concentrations (holes, notches)
- Interlaminar shear stresses
- Through-thickness tensile stresses

### Structural Significance

Delamination is particularly damaging because:
- It significantly reduces load-carrying capacity
- Creates buckling problems in compression
- Can grow under cyclic loading
- Difficult to detect without specialized inspection methods
- Requires both strength-based and fracture mechanics analysis for prediction

## Interaction Between Mechanisms

The damage modes seen in composites are interactive, where the development of one damage mechanism can delay or intensify the development of others, such as:

- Stress relaxation of matrix cracking affecting fibre failure onset
- Structural deformation of delamination buckling promoting matrix cracking
- Fibre failure triggering rapid delamination propagation
- Matrix cracking providing pathways for moisture ingress

This interactive nature makes composite failure prediction inherently complex and necessitates consideration of the composite as a structure rather than a simple material.
