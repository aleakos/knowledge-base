## Abstract

The paper investigates thick-walled composite pipes subjected to simultaneous multi-axial loads common to coiled tubing applications. The pipes were assumed to be filament wound carbon fiber-epoxy laminates with multiple layers and variable fiber orientations. MATLAB-based software was used to calculate stresses and carry out failure analyses through the thickness of the pipes when subjected to pressure, axial and bending (spooling) loads. Analyses were performed to determine if the composite tubes could achieve comparable strengths and spooling diameters as equivalent steel tube geometries.

## Introduction

Composites are increasingly recognized for their suitability in demanding applications across industries. Key advantages include:

- High specific strength-to-weight ratio
- Resistance to fluids (seawater, aerated water, hydrocarbons)
- Good thermal insulation
- Excellent damping and fatigue performance
- High strength and flexibility

The tailorability of fiber-reinforced composite structures (stacking sequence, fiber volume ratio, etc.) combined with superior load path characteristics has motivated industry adoption for critical load-bearing applications, particularly risers, spoolable tubulars, and tethers.

### Advantages Over Steel Tubing

With increasing borehole lengths, steel coiled tubing faces significant challenges:
- Weight increases as wall thickness must increase to support self-weight
- Large spooling strains push steel beyond its yield point, leading to low cycle use
- Limited spooling flexibility

Composite tubes offer advantages:
- Lower density and higher strength
- Extended working depths
- Greater flexibility during spooling

### Design Challenges for Composites

While composites offer advantages, disadvantages include:
- Breakage of inner fibers or crazes forming, leading to future fiber cracking
- Matrix cracking during use (requiring thermoplastic liner implementation)
- Cannot be pushed beyond yield point (unlike ductile steel)

### Primary Loads in Coiled Tubing

**Installation loads:**
- Bending around reel during spooling/unspooling

**Operational loads:**
- Axial loads
- Internal/external pressure after downhole deployment

**Secondary loads:**
- Thermal expansion/contraction
- Local radial compression
- Impact loading

### Manufacturing Considerations

Coiled tubes are thick-walled pipes of several kilometers length, requiring continuous manufacturing processes such as:
- Pultrusion
- Pull winding
- Pull braiding techniques

### Scope of Current Work

This paper investigates thick-walled composite pipes subjected to multi-axial loads common to coiled tubing applications. Previous studies have examined composite pipes in various loading scenarios; this work extends those analyses by considering:
- Variable fiber orientation angles
- Multiple pipe diameters and wall thicknesses
- Combined pressure and axial loading
- Bending loads for spooling diameter determination
- Through-thickness stress distribution
