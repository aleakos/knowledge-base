# The Piston Force Approach and Axial Tension Definition

## Two Schools of Buoyancy Determination

In the oil industry there are two "schools" of determining buoyancy:

1. **Archimedes' Principle** - Based on displaced fluid weight
2. **Piston Force Method (Force-Area Method)** - Based on force balance at size transitions

These two approaches give the same pipe weight at surface but may give different loadings throughout the drill string. Considerable confusion exists in the oil industry regarding which criterion is correct.

## Piston Force Methodology

The piston force is obtained by setting up a force balance. At each size transition a force is obtained equal to the pressure multiplied by the exposed area. This is a one-dimensional approach. 

**Critical Point:** If the stability force is subtracted from this method, it yields the same result as the Archimedes' principle. If this correction is not performed, the approach is wrong.

## Definition of Correct Axial Tension

There has been considerable debate over the years about the definition of the correct axial tension. 

- **Force-Area Method Advocates** argue that the "real" string tension is given by the summation of forces in the axial direction
- **Archimedes' Principle Advocates** argue that they calculate the "effective" tension

However, if the "fictitious" force (also called stability force or buckling force) is subtracted from the force-area result, both methods obtain the same result. This is the correct application of the force-area method.

## Tension Definition Based on Failure Criterion

The correct definition of tension depends on the failure criterion chosen because axial load is mainly used in failure assessments. 

As pure hydrostatic (isotropic) loading of a tubular does not lead to failure, it is of no interest. However, failure of metals is governed by distortion. Therefore, the key principle is:

**The correct definition of tension depends on the failure criterion chosen, since we mainly use axial load in failure assessments.**

This reframing shifts the debate from abstract definitions to practical engineering based on what causes actual pipe failure.
