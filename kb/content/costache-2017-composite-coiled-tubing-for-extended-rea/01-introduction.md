## Introduction

Coiled tubing is used for both drilling and completing oil wells. In horizontal sections, it performs fracture stimulation, wellbore cleaning, and runs perforating guns. These tubulars can be several kilometers long and are rolled onto large diameter reels. After unrolling and strengthening, the coiled tubing (CT) is pushed through vertical, build, and horizontal wellbore sections.

### Friction and Buckling Limitations

The primary limitation of conventional steel coiled tubing is friction. Over large distances, friction between wellbore casing and tubular creates large axial compressive loads. These loads result in:

1. Sinusoidal buckling at initial stages
2. Helical buckling at higher loads
3. Complete inability to push the tubular further

This buckling phenomenon fundamentally limits the horizontal reach achievable with steel systems.

### Previous Composite Coiled Tubing Attempts

**Anaconda System (late 1990s):** Jointly developed by Halliburton and Fiberspar Spoolable Products with Statoil support to exploit inaccessible reserves in North Sea fields. The system featured carbon fiber epoxy overwrap with thermoplastic inner liner but failed due to:
- Bonding problems between liner and epoxy
- Liner collapse
- Microcracking and brittle matrix fractures

**Airborne Oil and Gas System:** Successfully tested a thermoplastic CCT reinforced with glass fibers with Chevron and Maersk Oil involvement. Key findings:
- Good fatigue and collapse pressure performance
- Fully bonded tubular using melt-fused fiber-tapes
- Upper temperature limit expected below 80°C

**Ziebel Z-System:** Carbon epoxy rod composite with:
- External diameter: 15 mm
- Maximum production length: 6600 m
- Performance: 359 fatigue cycles at 35 MPa collapse pressure and 80°C
- Limitation: Cracks observed in circumferential hoop layer due to bending

### Composite Advantages

Despite lower bending stiffness compared to steel, composite coiled tubing achieves reach extension through:
- Lower submerged weight
- Reduced coefficient of friction
- Higher stiffness-to-weight ratio

This study demonstrates that composite systems are vastly superior to steel counterparts and will become the industry standard.
