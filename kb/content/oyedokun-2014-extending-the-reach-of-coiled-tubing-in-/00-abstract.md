# Abstract

This thesis investigates the limitations of coiled tubing (CT) reach in directional and extended-reach wells and proposes a novel technique to overcome those limitations.

Coiled tubing technology has become one of the most versatile intervention and drilling platforms in the oil and gas industry. However, its application in directional and extended-reach drilling is severely restricted by the inability to transmit torque and the tendency of the CT string to buckle under compressive loads in inclined wellbores.

The proposed technique introduces a **second downhole motor** placed in the CT string above the bit assembly. This second motor rotates the lower CT segment (between the two motors), converting a portion of the string from a nonrotating to a rotating configuration. By rotating the lower segment, frictional drag forces are redistributed — axial drag is reduced and replaced by a smaller torsional drag — thereby enabling CT to reach greater measured depths.

The thesis develops the mechanical model for both the rotating and nonrotating segments of the CT string. It analyzes:

- Kinematics of the rotating CT segment under combined axial and torsional loads
- Torsional buckling behavior in vertical, inclined, and curved wellbore sections
- The maximum allowable rotating length before torsional buckling initiates
- The role of a **dynamic torque arrestor** (a newly proposed tool) in limiting reactive torque transmitted to the nonrotating segment
- Axial force distribution models for CT under sinusoidal and helical buckling
- Maximum achievable measured depth under zero-hookload and lockup conditions

Numerical worked examples demonstrate that the proposed configuration significantly extends CT reach compared to the conventional nonrotating CT string.
