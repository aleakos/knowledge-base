# Chapter I: Introduction and Background

## 1.1 Coiled Tubing Technology

### Definition and Structure

Coiled tubing (CT) is a continuous, jointless steel tube typically ranging from 1 inch to 4.5 inches in outside diameter, spooled on a large reel at surface. Unlike conventional jointed drill pipe, CT is run into the well continuously without making or breaking threaded connections.

The CT surface equipment package includes:
- **Reel**: Large drum that stores the CT string
- **Injector head**: Applies the push/pull force to run or retrieve CT
- **Control cabin**: Houses instrumentation and controls
- **Wellhead equipment**: Stripper/BOP stack for pressure containment

### Evolution of Coiled Tubing

CT technology was first used commercially in the early 1960s, initially for nitrogen kick-off and cleanout operations. Advances in steel metallurgy, tube manufacturing, and downhole tool design have dramatically expanded CT applications. By the early 2000s, CT was used for:

- Well cleanouts and scale removal
- Perforating and acidizing
- Logging and production logging
- Drilling (CT drilling)
- Through-tubing rotary drilling (TTRD)

### Coiled Tubing Drilling

CT drilling uses a downhole motor (PDM or turbodrill) to rotate the bit while the CT string remains nonrotating. This allows directional work through bent housings without rotating the entire CT string. However, the absence of CT string rotation creates several critical limitations:

1. **Buckling under compression**: CT buckles helically at lower compressive loads than rotating drill pipe because it cannot transmit torque from surface
2. **Friction lockup**: Accumulated contact forces cause CT to lock up, preventing further axial advancement
3. **No weight transfer**: Helically buckled CT cannot transfer additional weight-on-bit beyond lockup
4. **No torque capability**: CT cannot transmit rotational torque from surface to the bit
5. **Casing wear risk**: Rotating CT would require careful management to avoid wear

### Coiled Tubing Advantages

Despite limitations in extended-reach applications, CT offers important operational advantages:
- Continuous pipe: No connections to make, faster tripping
- Live-well intervention: Operations under pressure without killing the well
- Compact footprint: Suitable for offshore platforms and remote locations
- High automation: Less personnel exposure
- Underbalanced drilling: CT can be used with lightweight fluids more easily

### Reach Limitation Problem

The core problem addressed by this thesis: in deviated or horizontal wells, CT reach is limited by:

- **Helical buckling lockup**: Beyond a critical compressive load, CT locks up in the wellbore and cannot advance
- **Zero-hookload condition**: When all available string weight is consumed by friction, the hookload falls to zero
- **Reactive torque**: The downhole motor generates torque that is reacted on the CT string, causing undesirable rotation or twist

These limitations severely restrict the maximum measured depth (MD) achievable with CT, particularly in long laterals and extended-reach wells.
