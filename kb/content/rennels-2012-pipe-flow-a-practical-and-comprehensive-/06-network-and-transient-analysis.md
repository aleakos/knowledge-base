## Network and Transient Analysis

### Series Flow

In series flow, the same mass flow passes through each component. Pressure drops are additive:
```
delta_P_total = sum(delta_P_i)
```
Referred to a single reference area using the K normalisation formula.

### Parallel Flow

In parallel flow, the pressure drop across each branch is equal:
```
delta_P_branch_1 = delta_P_branch_2 = ... = delta_P_branch_n
```
Total flow is the sum of branch flows. Solution requires simultaneous solution of pressure and continuity equations.

### Branching Flow

At a tee junction, both continuity (flow conservation) and energy equations apply. The system of equations is set up as a network problem with nodes (junctions) and branches (pipe segments), solved iteratively.

**Example: Ring sparger** — multiple injection ports around a ring distribute flow to each port. Solved by writing continuity equations at each node and energy equations for each branch, then solving the resulting nonlinear system.

### Transient Analysis

For flows where flow rate varies with time, ordinary differential equations replace the steady-state algebraic equations. Key examples:

**Vessel drain time**: A vessel draining through a pipe. The driving head decreases as the vessel empties. Solution by separating variables or numerical integration:
```
A_vessel * dh/dt = -Q(h)
```

**Positive displacement pump with pulsating flow**: Time-stepping integration of the mass and energy equations over each pump stroke cycle.

**Time-step integration**: General numerical method for any transient problem. Split time into small steps; at each step, compute flow rate and update pressures and fluid levels.

### Uncertainty Analysis (Chapter 7)

Uncertainty in pipe flow calculations comes from:
- Pipe roughness (±factor of 2 or more for aged pipe)
- Loss coefficient data (±10–20% typical)
- Fluid properties (density, viscosity)
- Measurement errors in pressure or flow rate

Uncertainty in pressure drop and flow rate are estimated by root-sum-square combination of individual uncertainty contributions.

### Cavitation (Chapter 20)

When local static pressure falls below the vapor pressure of a liquid, cavitation (local boiling) occurs. This can damage equipment and reduce flow capacity.

**Key concept — Net Positive Suction Head (NPSH)**:
```
NPSH = (P_inlet / rho_w) + V_inlet^2/(2g) - P_vapor/rho_w
```

The available NPSH must exceed the required NPSH of the pump to avoid cavitation. Pipe friction and fittings losses between the supply reservoir and the pump suction reduce available NPSH.

### Flow-Induced Vibration (Chapter 21)

- **Steady internal flow**: turbulence excites pipe vibration; critical where natural frequencies are near vortex shedding frequencies
- **Water hammer**: pressure wave generated by sudden valve closure
  ```
  a = sqrt(B / rho) / sqrt(1 + B*D/(E*t))
  ```
  where B = bulk modulus, E = pipe material modulus, t = wall thickness
- **Column separation**: vapor pocket forms when pressure drops to vapor pressure; violent pressure surge when cavity collapses

### Temperature Rise (Chapter 22)

Mechanical energy dissipated as heat in flowing fluids raises fluid temperature:
```
delta_T = HL * g / cp
```
where cp = specific heat at constant pressure. Usually negligible for water but significant for viscous fluids or high-pressure drops.

### Flow to Run Full (Chapter 23)

Horizontal openings at low flow rates: whether the pipe runs full or partially full depends on the Froude number. For Fr < ~0.8, the pipe may not run full, requiring open-channel flow analysis.
