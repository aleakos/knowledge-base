## Overview

This case illustrates the importance of modeling the job prior to mobilizing equipment. Three progressively more complicated attempts with 1.25-inch tubing were shown to fail prior to success with an easily executed job using 1.75-inch CT. This case demonstrates how the solids transport model improved understanding of cleanout operations.

## Well Geometry

### Formation and Completion

- Location: U.S.A.
- Formation: Vertical well with 25-degree deviated section at end
- Completion: 4-inch casing to 7550-ft
- Perforations: At 7550-ft
- Fill above perforations: Expected 150-ft after over-displaced fracture stimulation

### Initial Assessment

Because the well was almost vertical, a 1.25-inch CT unit was mobilized. An old rule of thumb suggested water at 20 inches/sec would be sufficient to clean the well.

## Attempt 1: Standard Water Circulation

### Job Program

- RIH at 2-ft/min to top of perforations at 7533-ft
- Circulate N₂ at 400 scfm and water at 0.5 bpm for two hole volumes
- Pump 20 bbl modified Xanthan polymer pill, displace with water at 1.1 bpm
- **Total job time: 6.57 hours**

### Results

The outcome shown in Figure 7 (solids percentage of completion cross-section):

- Each stage moved solids uphole
- **Failure**: All solids still remained in the 25-degree section of the well
- Pumping stopped and after 0.5 hours waiting, CT was RIH to find all fill still above perforations

### Root Cause

The flow rates were insufficient to lift solids in the deviated section before they settled. The lower fluid velocity meant sediment settled back even as circulation occurred.

## Attempt 2: Higher Water Rate

### Job Program

- RIH to bottom of perforations at 7550-ft
- Circulate water at 1.5 bpm for 5 hours (higher than Attempt 1)
- POH to 6000-ft and after 1 hour waiting, RIH again
- **Total job time: 6.25 hours**

### Results

The outcome shown in Figure 8:

- Higher flow rate was more effective at displacing solids
- **Failure**: Still failed to remove them in 6.25 hours
- Sand was tagged at 7488-ft (solids still above perforations)
- Progress was made but insufficient velocity sustained throughout the deviated section

### Lesson Learned

Even with higher flow rates, sustained solids transport velocity was not maintained throughout the longer circulation time required.

## Attempt 3: Foam and Polymer Combination

### Job Program

- RIH to 7550-ft
- Circulate foam, water at 0.1 bpm and N₂ at 550 scfm for 45 min
- Increase foam rate: water at 0.25 bpm and N₂ at 500 scfm for 15 min
- Pump 20 bbl modified Xanthan polymer pill at 1.0 bpm then water for 1 hour
- **Pump failure** during water stage, restarted after 30 min
- Circulation continued at 1.5 bpm for 2 hours
- POH to 6300-ft, waited 1 hour, RIH and tagged fill at 7492-ft
- **Total job time**: Extended due to pump failure

### Results

The outcome shown in Figure 9:

- Foam was assumed to entrain solids without dropping before gel pill
- **Failure**: Because top of fill did not change, solids must have fallen out during the 0.5-hour pump downtime
- Final high-rate water phase was too short to get all solids to surface

### Critical Observations

1. The pump failure during the operation disrupted momentum and allowed solids to settle
2. 0.5 hours of static conditions was sufficient for settled solids to require re-mobilization
3. Intermittent circulation patterns were ineffective at sustained solids transport

## Successful Operation: 1.75-inch CT Solution

### Equipment Change

The following week, another 1.75-inch CT unit was brought to location.

### Job Program

- RIH to 7550-ft at 2.5 bpm
- Left to circulate well for 2 hours
- **Total job time: 2 hours circulation plus trip time**

### Results

The outcome shown in Figure 10:

- **Success**: RIH check proved well was cleaned as predicted by software
- All 150-ft of fill was successfully removed
- Solids distribution show complete removal above perforations

### Key Difference

The larger 1.75-inch CT workstring provided:

- Higher carrying capacity for solids
- Better fluid velocity distribution in the deviated section
- More stable annular flow patterns
- Sustained solids transport throughout the operation

## Software Prediction Value

### Pre-Modeling Benefits

The critical lesson was **the importance of modeling the job prior to mobilizing equipment**. Software analysis could have predicted:

1. The 1.25-inch CT configuration would be marginal at best
2. The rule-of-thumb 20 inches/sec criterion was insufficient for this well geometry
3. A larger diameter string was needed to reliably achieve 100% solids removal
4. Continuous circulation without interruptions was critical

### Cost and Efficiency Impact

Proper pre-job modeling would have:

- Avoided three failed attempts (6.57 + 6.25 + extended hours = significant cost)
- Eliminated equipment remobilization costs
- Identified the optimum CT diameter from the start
- Provided confidence in the final operation plan

## Lessons and Conclusions

### Technical Insights

1. **Geometry matters**: Deviated sections require higher carrying velocities than vertical sections
2. **Continuous flow**: Interruptions allow settled solids to consolidate and require re-mobilization
3. **Diameter impact**: Larger CT provides better annular flow characteristics and solids carrying capacity
4. **Velocity threshold**: 20 inches/sec rule-of-thumb was inadequate for this application

### Operational Impact

1. **Pre-job modeling** is essential for complex cleanout operations
2. **Solids transport software** provides quantitative predictions superior to historical rules-of-thumb
3. **Equipment selection** should be based on modeling predictions, not just well depth/diameter
4. **Operational discipline** (avoiding interruptions) is critical for successful cleanouts

### Industry Value

This case history demonstrates the significant economic value of using CT software models during the pre-planning stage to:

- Select appropriate equipment (CT size)
- Design optimal circulation programs
- Predict cleanout success before mobilization
- Avoid costly operational failures
- Reduce overall well intervention costs and timeline
