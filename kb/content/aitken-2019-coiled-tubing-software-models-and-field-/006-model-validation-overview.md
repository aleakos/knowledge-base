## Overview

The models described in the CT software package have been used for back-calculating thousands of CT operations over the last 30 years. Currently, parameters for all CT operations around the world are predicted in the pre-job planning stage. In many of those operations, the mathematical models have been validated in the post-job stage by comparing pre-job predictions and actual post-job data.

## Default Coefficient of Friction Validation

### Initial Study (Craig, 2003)

A validation example was first presented by Craig (2003) who reviewed actual and predicted weight data from 33 7-inch Monobore oil producers in the Norwegian sector of the North Sea. The conclusion was that a constant coefficient of friction of 0.24 was representative for most of those wells, independent of:

- Well deviation complexity
- Pressure and production rates
- CT size
- CT sliding direction (RIH or POH)

This conclusion contrasted with prior published data which suggested that higher coefficients of friction were required for build sections.

The performed jobs included:
- Perforating and packer setting operations
- Sand cleanouts
- Milling
- Under-reaming operations

CT workstrings used were 2-inch, 2.375-inch, and 2.875-inch with wall thicknesses between 0.109-inch and 0.134-inch and 100,000 psi material yield strength.

### Weight Matching Process

The actual data from each run (RIH or POH) was implemented in the CT software model. The process involved:

1. Using the flow model to calculate CT frictional pressure drop effect on well pressure at discretized nodes
2. Using predicted pressures as input to the TFA model for force balance at each depth
3. Calculating operating limit and friction lock limit for RIH at each depth with various friction coefficients
4. Visually comparing output weight plots to see which coefficient best matched actual data
5. Achieving the match when changes in predicted to actual weights were parallel

The weight matching process was repeated until actual coefficients of friction were as close as possible for both RIH and POH.

### Coefficient Update

Because of Craig's review study, the default coefficient of friction was increased from 0.20 (used until approximately 2003 for all proprietary CT operations) to 0.24.

### Recent Confirmation

Recent studies on linear friction testing have confirmed that the default coefficient of friction of 0.24 recommended for use in the model is close to actual values. With the recent introduction of a tension, compression, and torque model to real-time telemetry, prediction improvements in the static and dynamic coefficients of friction can be refined further.

## Reduced Coefficient of Friction Validation

Several validation examples have been recently presented for CT operations with lubricants and fluid hammer tools in extended-reach wells.

### Laboratory Testing Scale

More than 12,000 laboratory tests were performed with:
- More than 20 proprietary and commercial lubricants
- Different types and sizes of CT coupons and casing samples
- Many base fluids
- Clean surfaces and surfaces covered in silica sand

Based on these tests, it was concluded that the coefficient of friction strongly depends on:
- Temperature
- Contact surface roughness
- Fluid chemistry

### Temperature-Dependent Friction

A simple relationship between temperature and coefficient of friction was proposed:

$$\mu = a + bT$$

where μ is the coefficient of friction, T is temperature, and a and b are fluid-dependent empirical coefficients. Note that if the temperature gradient is geothermal, the coefficient of friction varies linearly with depth.

### Optimal Lubricant Volume

A method was proposed to calculate the optimal volume of lubricant for the best lubricity, depending on the contact surface roughness.

### Fluid Hammer Tool Effects

For fluid hammer tools, a model was developed for predicting the effect of axial and radial vibrations generated by these tools on the coefficient of friction. It was shown that the coefficient of friction varies along the CT length, from the smallest value close to the fluid hammer tool to the largest value closer to the surface where the radial vibrations are damped.

### Extended-Reach Validation

The coefficients of friction dependence on temperature, contact surface roughness, and radial vibrations are implemented in the CT software model. After specifying the well trajectory, casing and CT materials, geothermal gradient, and if a fluid hammer tool is used, the coefficient of friction is implicitly calculated along the entire CT length. Then, an apparent coefficient of friction is calculated as an average value along the entire CT length.

Very good validation results were reported when comparing theoretical and laboratory coefficients of friction with their field counterparts. For instance, one study presented four extended-reach annular fracturing case histories from the U.S.A. for which predicted and actual coefficients of friction were between 0.09 and 0.14 during RIH and POH, with an average value of 0.12. For all four case histories, the differences between predicted and actual weights were less than 5%.

## Validation Summary Results

### Field Prediction Accuracy

- Flow model operational results have been within 5% from model predictions
- TFA validation: weight differences less than 5% for extended-reach operations with reduced friction coefficients
- Solids cleanout: 100% successful cleaning in 50-well examination campaign
- Operations predictions made with high confidence globally

### Key Validation Milestones

1. **1990s** - Horizontal logging operations provided field data for force model validation
2. **2001** - Enhanced engineered cleanout process introduced and validated against ultrasonic field data
3. **2003** - Craig's comprehensive study validated default friction coefficient of 0.24
4. **2008+** - Extensive validation of reduced friction coefficient operations with lubricants
5. **2015+** - Real-time telemetry integration enabling dynamic validation and model refinement

## Areas of Continuing Improvement

Key areas where model improvements based on field data continue:

- Accurate bottom hole pressure determination
- Gains or losses from formation characterization
- Integration of real-time telemetry data for dynamic coefficient of friction refinement
- Improved prediction of friction lock conditions
- Better modeling of complex multi-phase flow regimes in extended-reach wells
